## CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Background of the Study
Social media platforms have become deeply integrated into the daily life of university students. Platforms such as WhatsApp, Facebook, X (Twitter), Telegram, Instagram, and TikTok are now used not only for social interaction but also for communication related to coursework, group assignments, and access to educational content. For many students, social media has transformed from optional entertainment into a central communication infrastructure that affects learning routines, attention patterns, and time management.

At the same time, higher education institutions are concerned about fluctuations in student academic performance. Some students effectively use social media to exchange notes, participate in class groups, and access academic communities. Other students experience frequent interruptions, reduced concentration, sleep disturbances, and procrastination related to high social media engagement. These contrasting outcomes suggest that social media usage may influence academic performance in both positive and negative ways depending on usage pattern and purpose.

In Rwanda, smartphone penetration and affordable internet bundles have accelerated student access to digital platforms. Within Mount Kigali University, social media tools are visible in nearly all student activities, including formal academic engagements, informal peer support, and non-academic entertainment. Despite this reality, many policy and student support interventions still focus on traditional learning barriers while giving limited structured attention to digital behavior as a determinant of performance.

Recent institutional conversations around learner success, digital wellness, and online learning readiness indicate a need for empirical understanding of how social media behavior links to outcomes such as coursework completion, class participation, and grade trends. Without such evidence, administrators, lecturers, and student leaders may rely on assumptions when designing guidelines for digital use. A context-specific study is therefore necessary to generate practical evidence from Mount Kigali University.

This concept note proposes to examine the influence of social media usage on academic performance among undergraduate students. The study is expected to provide evidence that can support student counseling services, academic advising, and digital literacy programming while helping students adopt healthier and academically productive online habits.

### 1.2 Problem Statement (Three Parts)
The first part of the problem is that social media usage among undergraduate students has increased rapidly and now occupies substantial daily study time. Students use multiple platforms for communication, entertainment, and information sharing, often switching repeatedly between academic and non-academic content. As a result, learning time may be fragmented, attention may decline, and task completion may be delayed.

The second part is that academic performance among undergraduates remains uneven despite expansion of learning resources and communication technologies. While some students maintain strong grades, others report missed deadlines, poor revision habits, low concentration, and reduced lecture engagement. The link between these outcomes and social media behavior is frequently discussed but insufficiently measured in a structured local study.

The third part is the evidence and policy gap. At Mount Kigali University, limited institution-specific data exists on how intensity, purpose, and timing of social media usage influence academic performance. Without reliable evidence, interventions by lecturers, academic mentors, and student affairs units may be poorly targeted. This creates a practical need for research that identifies usage patterns and their relationship with performance indicators in order to guide context-relevant academic support strategies.

### 1.3 Purpose of the Study
The purpose of this study is to examine how social media usage influences academic performance among undergraduate students at Mount Kigali University.

### 1.7 Significance of the Study

- **Non-academic social media use:** platform use for entertainment, casual interaction, trend consumption, and unrelated browsing.
- **Academic performance indicators:** self-reported trends in grades, completion of assignments, participation in lectures/tutorials, and consistency of revision routines.
- **Time management behavior:** extent to which students plan study sessions, follow schedules, limit interruptions, and complete tasks within planned time.

## CHAPTER TWO: REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE

### 2.1 Introduction
This chapter reviews empirical and theoretical literature related to social media usage and academic performance. The review synthesizes available evidence, identifies converging and diverging findings, and establishes the research gap that the current study seeks to address.

### 2.2 Empirical Literature

#### 2.2.1 Intensity of Social Media Use and Academic Outcomes
Empirical studies from different university contexts report mixed findings on intensity of social media use. Several studies show that high daily exposure to non-academic social media content is associated with reduced concentration and lower grade outcomes. Continuous notifications and multitasking behavior can increase cognitive switching costs, resulting in shallow engagement with learning tasks.

Other studies report neutral or positive associations where students use social media for collaborative learning, peer tutoring, and rapid clarification of course content. In such contexts, usage intensity alone is not the strongest predictor of performance; the purpose and structure of usage matter more. When high-frequency usage is academically directed, outcomes may improve through increased access to support and learning communities.

A recurring limitation in this literature is overreliance on broad screen-time indicators without separating educational and entertainment components. This limitation reduces precision when interpreting the effect of usage intensity.

#### 2.2.2 Purpose of Social Media Use
Evidence suggests that purpose-specific usage is central to outcome variation. Academic-oriented use, such as participation in course groups, exchange of reading materials, and discussion of assignments, is often linked to better preparedness and confidence in coursework. Students frequently report that such use improves access to timely information and peer clarification.

Entertainment-dominant use, especially when extended into late-night periods, is commonly associated with fatigue, missed deadlines, and weaker revision discipline. Studies also indicate that passive scrolling and algorithm-driven content consumption may erode planned study blocks, even when students intend to use platforms briefly.

Existing research demonstrates that students often operate in blended modes where academic and non-academic use overlap. This overlap creates a practical challenge for intervention design, as students may begin with academic intent but drift into unrelated content.

#### 2.2.3 Time Management and Procrastination
A strong empirical theme across studies is the role of time management as a mediating factor between social media use and academic outcomes. Students with structured schedules and self-regulation strategies are less likely to experience negative effects from social media. In contrast, students lacking clear study routines often report prolonged online sessions that displace revision time.

Research on procrastination indicates that social media can function as an avoidance mechanism during demanding academic tasks. Short breaks may become extended disengagement periods, reducing study continuity and increasing anxiety near submission deadlines. Consequently, performance problems may emerge less from platform presence itself and more from unregulated usage behavior.

#### 2.2.4 Student Collaboration and Peer Learning
Some studies highlight beneficial roles of social media in collaborative learning environments. Group chats and online communities can improve peer accountability, resource sharing, and discussion momentum beyond classroom hours. Students in large classes may find social media groups particularly useful for quick problem-solving and reminders.

However, collaboration spaces can also become noisy, with misinformation, off-topic discussions, and social pressure that distracts from academic priorities. This dual pattern reinforces the need to examine platform use quality, not merely quantity.

#### 2.2.5 African and Regional University Contexts
In many African universities, social media plays a significant role where formal digital learning infrastructure may be uneven. Messaging applications frequently substitute for official learning management channels. While this can increase inclusivity and responsiveness, it can also blur boundaries between social and academic life.

Regional studies recommend context-specific evidence because digital behavior is shaped by local cost of data, device access, campus culture, and institutional communication norms. Findings from non-African contexts may not transfer directly. This creates a clear rationale for a focused study at Mount Kigali University.

### 2.3 Theoretical Literature

#### 2.3.1 Uses and Gratifications Theory
Uses and Gratifications Theory explains media behavior as audience-driven, where users actively select media to satisfy specific needs such as information, social interaction, entertainment, or identity expression. Applied to this study, undergraduate students engage social media for varying gratifications, and these motivations shape outcomes. Academic gratification-seeking may support performance, while entertainment-driven overuse may weaken study discipline.

#### 2.3.2 Social Cognitive Theory
Social Cognitive Theory emphasizes reciprocal interaction among behavior, personal factors, and environment. Students observe peer behaviors online, model habits, and adjust their own routines accordingly. If peer groups normalize productive academic sharing, performance may improve. If peer culture rewards constant online presence and distraction, performance may decline.

#### 2.3.3 Self-Regulated Learning Theory
Self-Regulated Learning Theory is highly relevant because it addresses planning, monitoring, and control of learning activities. Students who set clear goals, manage time, and monitor digital interruptions can use social media in supportive ways. Students with weak self-regulation are more vulnerable to procrastination and fragmented study sessions.

### 2.4 Synthesis and Research Gap
The literature indicates that social media can function as both a learning support and a distraction risk. Outcome direction depends on intensity, purpose, and self-regulatory behavior. Although this evidence is useful, there is still limited context-specific analysis for Mount Kigali University that combines these dimensions in one coherent framework linked to practical student support measures.

The current study addresses this gap by examining the relationship between social media usage patterns and academic performance among undergraduates within a defined institutional and temporal context.

### 2.5 Conceptual Framework (Narrative)
The conceptual framework positions social media usage (frequency, duration, purpose, timing) as the independent variable and academic performance indicators as the dependent variable. Time management and self-regulation are treated as key behavioral pathways through which social media usage influences performance outcomes.

### 2.6 Critique of Existing Literature
Although existing literature is useful in framing the present study, several limitations are evident.

First, many studies rely heavily on self-reported screen time without independent verification. Self-report tools are useful but can be affected by recall bias and social desirability effects. Some students may underreport entertainment use and overreport academic use, especially when they perceive academic use as socially approved behavior.

Second, several studies analyze social media as a single undifferentiated exposure variable. This approach overlooks major differences across platforms, interaction formats, and content types. For example, quick communication in class groups may have a different effect than endless short-video feeds. Without this differentiation, policy recommendations become too generic.

Third, cross-sectional designs dominate the field. Such designs identify associations but rarely establish directionality of effects. Lower-performing students may increase social media usage as a coping behavior, while high social media usage may also contribute to lower performance. Longitudinal designs are needed to clarify this direction over time.

Fourth, contextual underrepresentation remains a challenge in African higher education studies. Institutional communication systems, campus internet access patterns, and local student cultures shape digital behavior in ways that differ from settings where most literature originates. Localized studies are therefore necessary for policy relevance.

Fifth, limited integration of behavioral theories reduces explanatory power in some studies. Descriptive statistics alone may identify patterns but fail to explain why students maintain behaviors that conflict with academic goals. Combining empirical evidence with theory improves interpretation and intervention design.

### 2.7 Theoretical and Empirical Implications for the Current Study
The current study draws practical guidance from reviewed literature in three ways.

The first implication is measurement design. Since purpose matters, the instrument will capture not only time spent online but also academic versus non-academic usage patterns and context of use.

The second implication is analytical focus. Since time management appears to mediate outcomes, the analysis will include behavioral indicators of planning, interruption control, and deadline discipline.

The third implication is intervention relevance. The study will prioritize actionable findings for student support systems, moving beyond broad claims about social media harm or benefit.

## CHAPTER THREE: RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

### 3.1 Introduction
This chapter presents the methodology that will guide the study. It includes research design, study population, sample size and sampling, data collection instruments and procedures, data analysis methods, and ethical considerations.

### 3.2 Research Design (1/2 page)
The study will use a mixed-methods research design, integrating quantitative and qualitative approaches. The quantitative component will provide measurable trends on frequency and purpose of social media use and links to academic performance indicators. The qualitative component will capture student explanations, experiences, and contextual factors that cannot be fully represented through numeric responses alone.

A convergent design will be used where quantitative and qualitative data are collected during the same period, analyzed separately, and interpreted together. This design is suitable for developing both breadth and depth of understanding regarding the influence of social media behavior on learning outcomes.

### 3.3 Study Population/Respondents (1 page)
The study population will comprise undergraduate students enrolled at Mount Kigali University. The entire population includes all registered undergraduates across selected colleges in the study period.

The target population will include undergraduate students who actively use at least one social media platform and are willing to participate in the research process.

The sample population will be drawn from the target population using a statistically acceptable formula. For large populations, Yamane's formula can be applied as follows:

\[
n = \frac{N}{1 + N(e)^2}
\]

Where:
- \(n\) = sample size
- \(N\) = target population size
- \(e\) = margin of error (commonly 0.05)

Assuming a target population \(N = 10,000\) and \(e = 0.05\):

\[
n = \frac{10,000}{1 + 10,000(0.05)^2}
= \frac{10,000}{1 + 25}
= \frac{10,000}{26}
= 384.6
\]

The study will use an approximate sample size of 385 respondents for the survey component, with additional purposively selected participants for qualitative interviews.

### 3.4 Sampling Techniques Used (1/2 page)
The study will apply both purposive (non-random) and probability (random) sampling techniques.

Purposive sampling will be used to select key informants and student representatives with rich insight into student digital behavior and academic trends.

Simple random sampling will be used to select survey participants from eligible undergraduate lists where available. This approach improves representativeness and reduces selection bias.

Where complete subgroup lists are accessible, stratified random sampling may be applied by college or year of study to ensure balanced participation.

### 3.5 Research Instruments and Data Collection Tools (1/2 page)
The study will use the following instruments:
- Structured questionnaire for undergraduate respondents (quantitative data)
- Semi-structured interview guide for selected key informants (qualitative data)
- Document review checklist for relevant institutional policy and communication materials

Questionnaires will capture demographic details, frequency and purpose of social media usage, time management practices, and indicators of academic performance. Interview guides will explore perceptions of digital behavior, learning habits, and suggested interventions.

### 3.6 Data Collection Procedure
After obtaining administrative authorization, the researcher will pretest instruments with a small group of respondents to check clarity and reliability. Revisions will be made before full rollout.

Data collection will then proceed through physical and/or online survey distribution, followed by scheduled interviews with selected participants. Completed questionnaires will be checked for completeness before coding and entry.

### 3.7 Data Analysis Procedures (1/2 page)
Quantitative data will be coded and analyzed using descriptive and inferential statistics. Descriptive statistics will include frequencies, percentages, means, and standard deviations. Inferential analysis may include correlation and regression tests to determine relationships between social media usage variables and academic performance indicators.

Qualitative data from interviews will be transcribed and analyzed thematically. Themes will be developed around usage purpose, time management, motivation, and academic outcomes. Triangulation will be used to compare qualitative insights with survey findings.

### 3.8 Validity and Reliability
Content validity will be strengthened through supervisor review and alignment of instrument items with research objectives. A pilot test will be conducted to assess consistency and comprehension of questionnaire items.

Reliability for scaled items may be assessed using Cronbach's alpha. Necessary modifications will be made where item consistency is weak.

### 3.9 Ethical Considerations
The following ethical principles will guide the study:
- Informed consent: participants will receive clear information and voluntarily consent.
- Confidentiality: no identifying personal details will be disclosed in reporting.
- Privacy and anonymity: responses will be coded and stored securely.
- Non-maleficence: participants will not be exposed to avoidable harm.
- Right to withdraw: participants may withdraw at any stage without penalty.
- Data protection: collected data will be used strictly for academic purposes.

### 3.10 Study Limitations and Delimitations
The study may encounter several limitations. Self-reported performance and usage data may include personal perception errors. Access to complete official grade records may be constrained by confidentiality and administrative procedures. Time and resource constraints may also limit the number of qualitative interviews that can be conducted.

Despite these limitations, the study includes practical delimitations. It focuses on undergraduate students, selected social media usage dimensions, and a defined institutional setting within a specific time range. These delimitations improve feasibility while keeping the study aligned with its objectives.

### 3.11 Quality Control Measures
To improve data quality, the study will implement the following controls:
- Pretesting instruments to identify ambiguous items.
- Training data assistants on ethical conduct and standardized administration.
- Conducting routine spot-checks for questionnaire completeness.
- Using coded entry templates to minimize data input errors.
- Applying triangulation between quantitative and qualitative findings.

These measures are expected to improve reliability and internal consistency of findings.
